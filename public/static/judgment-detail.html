⚖️ Supreme Court of India

Vikram Enterprisesv.Union of India & Ors.

Civil Appeal No. 4512 of 2026 · Decided: April 17, 2026
Bench: 2 Judges
Result: Appeal Dismissed
Area: Tax Law / GST
Citation: 2026 SCC OnLine SC 1124
Reporting: Not Yet Reported
Parties
Appellant
Vikram Enterprises (P) Ltd.

Respondents

📋 Background & Facts

The appellant, Vikram Enterprises, received a notice of demand under the Central Goods and Services Tax Act, 2017 (CGST Act) for ₹4.2 crore in respect of alleged input tax credit (ITC) irregularities spanning the financial years 2018–19 and 2019–20. Pursuant to the CGST Act's adjudication process, the demand was confirmed by the Adjudicating Authority, and the appellant chose to file an appeal before the GST Appellate Authority.

Under Section 107(6) of the CGST Act, as amended by the Finance Act 2021, the appellant was required to deposit 10% of the disputed tax demand as a condition precedent for the appeal being entertained. This pre-deposit requirement was challenged by the appellant on the ground that it constituted an unconstitutional restriction on the right to appeal, violating Articles 14 (equality before law) and 19(1)(g) (right to carry on trade or business) of the Constitution.

The Bombay High Court dismissed the writ petition filed by the appellant, holding that the pre-deposit requirement was a valid regulatory measure and did not amount to an unreasonable restriction. The present appeal was filed against that judgment.

❓ Issues Framed
1
Whether the mandatory pre-deposit of 10% of the disputed tax demand under Section 107(6) of the CGST Act, 2017 is violative of Article 14 of the Constitution of India as being arbitrary and unreasonable?
2
Whether the said pre-deposit requirement constitutes an unreasonable restriction on the right to carry on trade and business under Article 19(1)(g) of the Constitution, not saved by Article 19(6)?
3
Whether the doctrine of legitimate expectation applies to tax statutes that are prospectively amended by Parliament to enhance the pre-deposit threshold?
⚖️ Key Holdings (All by Justices Oka & Agarwal)
H1
Pre-deposit does not violate Article 14. Parliament has the competence to prescribe conditions precedent for exercising a statutory right of appeal. The classification of appellants who have deposited 10% from those who have not bears a rational nexus to the object of ensuring that the revenue's legitimate interests are not frustrated by frivolous appeals. The doctrine of manifest arbitrariness cannot be invoked where the legislature has made a considered policy choice within its domain.
H2
No violation of Article 19(1)(g). The right to file an appeal is a creature of statute; it does not exist independently of the statutory framework. The pre-deposit is a reasonable restriction "in the interests of the general public" within the meaning of Article 19(6), as it serves the legitimate aim of filtering vexatious appeals and protecting government revenue.
H3
Doctrine of legitimate expectation inapplicable to tax statutes. An assessee cannot claim a legitimate expectation that the pre-deposit percentage will remain at the level applicable when it first commenced business or when the dispute arose. Tax law is an evolving regulatory domain, and Parliament's power to prospectively amend deposit requirements is well within its plenary legislative competence under Entries 82 and 84 of Union List.

✏️ LawBrief Commentary
⭐ Editorial Analysis

This judgment settles a question that had produced conflicting outcomes across multiple High Courts — Bombay, Allahabad, Gujarat and Madras had all examined pre-deposit challenges in GST matters with varying reasoning, though all ultimately upheld the requirement. The Supreme Court's consolidation of the doctrinal basis is therefore welcome, even if the outcome was unsurprising. The most significant aspect of the judgment is the Court's categorical rejection of the legitimate expectation argument, which had found some traction at the High Court level. Practitioners advising assessees in pending CGST appeals should note that there is now no constitutional basis for challenging the 10% pre-deposit, though challenges specific to cases where the demand itself is ex facie without jurisdiction may still survive under a different legal theory.
