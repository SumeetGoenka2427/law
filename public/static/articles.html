Opinion · Arbitration Law

Why India's Arbitration Regime Needs a Course Correction Before the Next Decade Passes It By

India's ambitious arbitration reforms — the Arbitration and Conciliation (Amendment) Acts of 2015, 2019 and 2021 — have created a framework that looks impressive on paper but stumbles repeatedly in practice. The mounting backlog at the Delhi International Arbitration Centre, the persistent enforcement bottlenecks at execution courts, and the silent brain drain of India's most talented arbitrators to Singapore and London demand urgent collective action from the legislature, the Supreme Court and the Bar Council of India.

Three structural failures are at the heart of the problem. First, the judiciary's interventionist instinct has not diminished despite statutory language designed to limit court involvement. Second, institutional arbitration centres continue to operate without adequate infrastructure, case management technology or transparent fee structures that would attract complex commercial disputes. Third, the absence of a specialised arbitration bar — with dedicated practitioners who develop genuine expertise rather than treating arbitration as an adjunct to litigation practice — means that the quality of advocacy in major arbitrations remains uneven.

Senior Advocate Ramesh Krishnamurthy
Chambers, Supreme Court of India · April 17, 2026
