Why India's Arbitration Regime Needs a Course Correction Before the Next Decade Passes It By

Three structural failures — judicial overreach, institutional inadequacy and an underdeveloped arbitration bar — are systematically eroding India's ambitions of becoming a global arbitration hub. The reforms needed are within reach, but the window is narrowing.

Senior Advocate Ramesh Krishnamurthy
Designated Senior Advocate, Supreme Court of India · Arbitration Specialist
April 17, 2026 8 min read 34,220 views
The Supreme Court of India — whose interventionist tendencies in arbitration matters remain a primary concern despite statutory reforms. | Photo: Unsplash

India's ambitious arbitration reforms — the Arbitration and Conciliation (Amendment) Acts of 2015, 2019 and 2021 — have created a framework that looks impressive on paper but stumbles repeatedly in the execution. The mounting backlog at the Delhi International Arbitration Centre, the persistent enforcement bottlenecks at execution courts, and the quiet departure of India's most talented arbitrators to Singapore and London demand urgent collective action from the legislature, the Supreme Court, and the Bar Council of India.

Let me be specific about what is failing, and why each failure matters to the clients and businesses that are supposed to benefit from faster, cheaper and more certain dispute resolution.

I. The Judiciary's Interventionist Instinct Has Not Diminished

The legislature's express intent in the 2015 amendment was to constrain court involvement in arbitration to the bare minimum mandated by the UNCITRAL Model Law. Section 5 of the Act was amended to read that judicial authority "shall not intervene except where so provided" in Part I. The legislative drafters believed this language was sufficiently clear. They were wrong.

Indian courts — at every level — have continued to intervene at virtually every stage of the arbitral process: before appointment of arbitrators (through proceedings under Section 9 that frequently become full-fledged mini-trials), during arbitration (through injunction applications dressed up as Section 9 petitions), in setting aside proceedings (where courts apply a far more expansive reading of "patent illegality" than Parliament intended), and at the enforcement stage (where judgments of foreign courts sitting in supervisory jurisdiction over arbitral awards are routinely characterised as "foreign decrees" rather than "foreign awards" to escape the New York Convention's compulsory enforcement regime).

"The problem is not that Indian judges are hostile to arbitration — most are not. The problem is institutional: appellate courts routinely reverse lower court decisions that correctly refuse to interfere with awards, which over time trains the entire system towards greater intervention."

— Senior Advocate M.P. Singh, Former Additional Solicitor General of India, in conversation with the author

The Supreme Court's own jurisprudence has been inconsistent. On one hand, decisions like PASL Wind Solutions v. GE Power (2021) and NV Investment Holdings v. Future Retail (2021) signalled a more pro-arbitration posture. On the other, the Court's subsequent decisions on "group of companies" doctrine, the arbitrability of fraud, and the scope of emergency arbitration have created fresh uncertainty precisely in the areas where international commercial parties most need predictability.

📋 Key Statistic

According to the Ministry of Law and Justice's 2025 Annual Report, India accounts for approximately 1.2% of international commercial arbitrations registered at the ICC, SIAC and LCIA combined — despite being the world's fifth-largest economy. Singapore alone accounts for over 12%.

II. Institutional Centres Remain Structurally Inadequate

India currently has four statutory arbitration institutions: the Indian Council of Arbitration (ICA), the International Centre for Alternative Dispute Resolution (ICADR), the Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration (MCIA), and the Delhi International Arbitration Centre (DIAC). None of these institutions can seriously compete with the SIAC, ICC or LCIA for complex, high-value commercial disputes.

The reasons are structural and have not been honestly addressed. First, the panel of arbitrators at most Indian institutions reflects the same judicial-heavy composition that characterises the general arbitration bar, without adequate representation from engineers, accountants, and domain specialists who are indispensable for complex construction, energy or IP disputes. Second, case management technology at Indian institutions remains years behind: document management systems, hearing scheduling platforms and award tracking databases that SIAC has had since 2015 are still absent at DIAC and MCIA. Third, and most critically, institutional rules have not been updated to reflect modern practice in multi-party disputes, emergency arbitration timelines, or the use of AI-assisted document review.

"Until Indian institutions can credibly offer what Singapore and London offer — speed, certainty, neutrality, and genuine expertise — sophisticated commercial parties will continue to choose foreign seats even for disputes that are entirely India-centric."

III. The Arbitration Bar Is Underdeveloped — And Everyone Is Pretending Otherwise

The most uncomfortable truth about Indian arbitration is that the specialist arbitration bar barely exists outside a handful of chambers in New Delhi and Mumbai. The overwhelming majority of advocates appearing in arbitrations — including large commercial ones — are litigators who have transposed courtroom habits and procedural instincts into a setting that demands an entirely different skill set.

Effective arbitration advocacy requires comfort with document-heavy proceedings, the ability to manage large bundles and chronologies efficiently, facility with expert evidence and cross-examination, and — above all — the discipline to write rather than speak. Indian advocates, trained in a tradition that prizes oral advocacy over written pleadings, frequently struggle in arbitrations where the award is almost entirely determined by the quality of written submissions.

What Needs to Change — Specifically

The path forward requires coordinated action across four fronts:

  • A constitutional bench decision on the scope of "patent illegality" under Section 34(2A), which should once and for all confirm that courts cannot re-examine the merits of a domestic award under the guise of identifying legal error on the face of the award.
  • Mandatory capacity-building for institutional arbitration centres, funded jointly by the Ministry of Law and the Ministry of Finance, modelled on the Singapore government's support for SIAC's infrastructure development in the 2000s.
  • A dedicated LLM programme in international commercial arbitration at the five National Law Schools with the highest CLAT scores, with a compulsory residency component requiring students to spend at least one semester at a foreign institution or international arbitral centre.
  • An amendment to Section 29A extending the default timeline for making an award from 12 to 18 months, while simultaneously creating a fast-track procedure for disputes below ₹5 crore with a mandatory 6-month timeline and written-only proceedings.

None of these measures requires new legislation in the immediate term — though the Section 29A amendment would benefit from it. The Supreme Court can address the first through its existing supervisory jurisdiction. The Ministry can address the second through executive action. The law schools can address the third with appropriate BCI support. These are not big-bang reforms; they are incremental, achievable, and overdue.

India's arbitration ecosystem has produced enough diagnoses. The deficit is not in analysis but in the institutional will to act before the window closes. If the legal profession and the government do not move decisively in the next three years, India risks entrenching its status as a junior partner in the global arbitration economy — a country that produces the disputes but exports the resolution.
